\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{LegalSum}, the first unsupervised multimodal framework for
automatic summarization of courtroom video recordings.
Unlike generic video summarization methods that operate on visual features alone,
courtroom proceedings present unique challenges: long duration (2--8 hours),
multi-speaker turn-taking, domain-specific acoustic prosody (objections, judicial
pronouncements), and the legal obligation to preserve narrative completeness across
proceeding phases. LegalSum addresses these challenges through six novel
architectural contributions: (1)~a \textit{SpectralLegalSaliencyEncoder} that
isolates legally-relevant prosodic frequencies via FFT-based MFCC filtering;
(2)~a \textit{CrossModalAttentionFusion} module with saliency-weighted
bidirectional cross-attention; (3)~a \textit{GatedTemporalRouting} mechanism
with multi-dimensional channel-wise gating; (4)~a \textit{TemporalSegmentGraph}
sparse $k$-NN graph for $O(Tk)$ long-range reasoning; (5)~a
\textit{ConversationalHypergraphAttention} module exploiting speaker-role and
event-phase structure; and (6)~a \textit{DualPathwayDSN} that soft-mixes
visual-heavy and audio-heavy pathways per frame. Training uses Proximal Policy
Optimization with a novel Optimal-Transport temporal diversity reward and a
three-phase curriculum. LegalSum achieves state-of-the-art performance among
unsupervised methods on SumMe (53.2\% F1) and TVSum (62.1\% F1) while receiving
significantly higher ratings from legal domain experts on narrative completeness
and phase coverage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The exponential growth of court recording archives presents a critical bottleneck
for the legal system. Existing video summarization
methods~\cite{zhang2016video,mahasseni2017unsupervised} are designed for
consumer videos and fail in courtroom settings for three reasons.

\textbf{(1) Domain mismatch.} Consumer video summarization targets aesthetically
salient moments. Legal summarization requires preserving procedurally salient
moments: admissions, objections, judicial rulings, and the complete narrative arc
across all proceeding phases.

\textbf{(2) Acoustic primacy.} In court video, the acoustic channel carries
legally critical information. The prosodic signature of an objection is a
reliable saliency signal that visual-only methods completely ignore.

\textbf{(3) Multi-speaker temporal structure.} Court proceedings follow strict
speaker-role protocols. Standard attention treats all frames as members of a
single homogeneous sequence, ignoring speaker-role structure.

We make the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{LegalSum}: the first unsupervised multimodal framework for
courtroom video summarization.
\item \textbf{Six novel components}: SpectralLegalSaliencyEncoder,
CrossModalAttentionFusion, GatedTemporalRouting, TemporalSegmentGraph,
ConversationalHypergraphAttention, and DualPathwayDSN.
\item \textbf{OT Temporal Diversity Reward}: a 1D Wasserstein reward that
enforces chronological coverage, closing a fundamental gap in existing
RL-based methods.
\item \textbf{Three-Phase Curriculum}: differentiable contrastive pre-training
followed by PPO-Clip phases with warm-start reward scheduling.
\item \textbf{State-of-the-art results} on SumMe and TVSum among all
unsupervised methods.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Video Summarization}

Early methods use handcrafted features~\cite{gygli2014creating,song2015tvsum}.
Deep learning approaches train supervised
regressors~\cite{zhang2016video,fajtl2018summarizing} or use unsupervised
generative objectives~\cite{mahasseni2017unsupervised}.
RL-based methods define rewards over summary quality without
labels~\cite{zhou2018deep}.
The strongest recent unsupervised results use LLM/VLM
zero-shot~\cite{narasimhan2023prompts}, but require billion-parameter models
unavailable in air-gapped legal archives.

\subsection{Graph-Based Summarization}

SumGraph~\cite{park2020sumgraph} builds visual similarity graphs.
A2Summ~\cite{he2023a2summ} proposes anchor-based attention for multi-source
alignment. These methods operate on general-domain videos and do not model
legal structure, speaker roles, or acoustic prosody.

\subsection{Legal Multimedia Processing}

NLP research on legal texts has produced extractive
summarizers~\cite{zhong2020iteratively}.
Video-based legal analysis is nascent: \citet{sharma2021multimodal} use
audio-visual features for verdict prediction but not summarization.
LegalSum is the first work to address unsupervised video summarization in
the courtroom domain.

\subsection{Optimal Transport in Vision}

OT has been applied to domain adaptation~\cite{flamary2016optimal}, visual
matching~\cite{sarlin2020superglue}, and generative
modeling~\cite{kolouri2019generalized}. We are the first to use 1D Wasserstein
distance as a temporal coverage reward for RL-based video summarization.

\section{Problem Formulation}

Let video $V$ consist of $T$ frames with visual features
$\mathbf{F} = \{\mathbf{f}_t\}_{t=1}^T$, $\mathbf{f}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_v}$,
acoustic features $\mathbf{A} = \{\mathbf{a}_t\}_{t=1}^T$,
$\mathbf{a}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_a}$, and semantic vectors
$\mathbf{S} = \{\mathbf{s}_t\}_{t=1}^T$.

A policy $\pi_\theta$ maps the multimodal input to per-frame importance
probabilities $\mathbf{p} \in (0,1)^T$. A binary summary mask
$\mathbf{y} \in \{0,1\}^T$ is obtained via 0/1 knapsack with 15\% budget.

\textbf{Objective:} Train $\pi_\theta$ without human labels to maximize a
composite reward $R(\mathbf{F}, \mathbf{y})$ capturing diversity, coverage,
temporal uniformity, and legal narrative coherence.

\subsection{Multi-Scale Visual Projection}

Visual features $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_v}$ pass through
\textit{MultiScaleConv1D}: three parallel 1-D convolutions with kernels
$k \in \{1,3,5\}$, concatenated and projected to $\mathbb{R}^{T \times 512}$.
This captures frame-local texture ($k=1$) and short-range motion patterns ($k=5$).

\subsection{NOVEL-1: SpectralLegalSaliencyEncoder}

Raw MFCC features treat all frequency bands equally. In courtroom proceedings,
legally salient acoustic events produce characteristic energy at specific
prosodic frequencies (1--8 Hz).

Given MFCC features $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times n_{\text{mfcc}}}$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{A}} = \text{rfft}(\mathbf{A}, \text{dim}=0), \quad
\tilde{\mathbf{A}} = \text{irfft}(\text{TopK}(|\hat{\mathbf{A}}|))
\end{equation}
A two-layer MLP maps band-passed features to per-frame saliency:
$\mathbf{g} = \sigma(\text{MLP}(\tilde{\mathbf{A}})) \in (0,1)^T$.
This provides a supervision-free acoustic saliency gate.

\subsection{NOVEL-2: CrossModalAttentionFusion}

Bidirectional cross-attention with spectral gating:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{A}' &= \mathbf{W}_a\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{g}^\top \\
\mathbf{C}_a &= \text{CrossAttn}(\mathbf{V}, \mathbf{A}'), \quad
\mathbf{C}_s = \text{CrossAttn}(\mathbf{V}, \mathbf{S}) \\
\boldsymbol{\alpha} &= \sigma(\mathbf{W}_g[\mathbf{C}_a;\mathbf{C}_s]) \\
\mathbf{H}_{\text{fused}} &= \boldsymbol{\alpha} \odot \mathbf{C}_a + (1-\boldsymbol{\alpha}) \odot \mathbf{C}_s
\end{align}
The learned gate $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ dynamically balances acoustic-fused vs.\
semantic-fused representations per frame.

\subsection{NOVEL-3: GatedTemporalRouting}

Prior work~\cite{zhou2018deep} uses a scalar gate to blend local (RNN) and
global (attention) context. A scalar gate is capacity-limited: all feature
dimensions are routed identically. We replace it with a $d$-dimensional
channel-wise gate:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{g}_r &= \sigma(\mathbf{W}_r[\mathbf{h}_{\text{rnn}};\mathbf{h}_{\text{attn}}]) \in (0,1)^d \\
\mathbf{h}_{\text{gtr}} &= \text{LN}(\mathbf{g}_r \odot \mathbf{h}_{\text{rnn}} +
(1-\mathbf{g}_r) \odot \mathbf{h}_{\text{attn}} + \text{MLP}(\cdot))
\end{align}

\subsection{NOVEL-4: TemporalSegmentGraph}

Full self-attention is $O(T^2)$ -- prohibitive for long court videos. We reduce
this to $O(Tk)$ via a sparse $k$-NN graph ($k=5$) with two rounds of attentive
message passing:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_i^{(l+1)} = \text{LN}\!\left(\mathbf{h}_i^{(l)} +
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} e_{ij} \mathbf{h}_j^{(l)}\right)
\end{equation}
where $e_{ij} = \text{softmax}_j(\text{MLP}([\mathbf{h}_i;\mathbf{h}_j]))$.
This propagates context between semantically similar frames regardless of
temporal distance at $20\times$ lower complexity than full attention.

\subsection{NOVEL-5: ConversationalHypergraphAttention}

Court proceedings are naturally partitioned into speaker-role hyperedges
(judge, prosecution, defense, witness) and event-phase hyperedges (opening,
examination, cross-examination, objection, ruling, closing). Standard graph
methods require explicit pairwise edges; hyperedges efficiently group frames
by shared set-level membership:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{e}_k &= \text{AttentiveAgg}(\{\mathbf{h}_i : i \in \mathcal{E}_k\}) \\
\mathbf{h}_i^{\text{hyper}} &= \text{LN}\!\left(\mathbf{h}_i +
\sum_{k: i \in \mathcal{E}_k} \text{Linear}(\mathbf{e}_k)\right)
\end{align}
This is, to our knowledge, the first hypergraph attention mechanism applied
to video summarization.

\subsection{DSN Backbone Forward Pass}

\begin{equation}
\small
\mathbf{F} \xrightarrow{\text{MultiScale}} \xrightarrow{\text{CrossModal}}
\xrightarrow{\text{BiLSTM}} \xrightarrow{\text{GTR+MHSA}}
\xrightarrow{\text{TSG}} \xrightarrow{\text{CHA}} \xrightarrow{\sigma} \mathbf{p}
\end{equation}

\subsection{NOVEL-6: DualPathwayDSN}

Legal videos are heterogeneous: exhibit display frames demand visual primacy;
judicial pronouncements demand acoustic primacy. A single DSN cannot adjust
modal emphasis per frame. We use two independent DSN instances:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{Visual pathway}: visual + semantic, dropout $=0.25$
\item \textit{Acoustic pathway}: visual + acoustic, dropout $=0.30$
\end{itemize}

A per-frame MLP mixer produces a soft combination:
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\alpha} = \sigma(\text{MLP}([\mathbf{p}_v;\mathbf{p}_a])), \quad
\mathbf{p}_{\text{out}} = \boldsymbol{\alpha} \odot \mathbf{p}_v + (1-\boldsymbol{\alpha}) \odot \mathbf{p}_a
\end{equation}

\section{Reward Design}

\subsection{Core 6-Component Reward}

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{core}} = w_{\text{div}}R_{\text{div}} + w_{\text{cov}}R_{\text{cov}} +
w_{\text{spr}}R_{\text{spr}} + w_{\text{cmp}}R_{\text{cmp}} +
w_{\text{nar}}R_{\text{nar}} + w_{\text{leg}}R_{\text{leg}}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Diversity} $R_{\text{div}}$: mean pairwise cosine dissimilarity of selected frames.
\textbf{Coverage} $R_{\text{cov}}$: submodular facility-location coverage.
\textbf{Spread} $R_{\text{spr}}$: variance of selected frame positions.
\textbf{Compactness} $R_{\text{cmp}}$: negative mean inter-selection gap.
\textbf{Narrative Flow} $R_{\text{nar}}$: cosine similarity of adjacent selected frames.
\textbf{Legal Density} $R_{\text{leg}}$: mean legal keyword density over selected frames.

\subsection{Reward Warm-Start Curriculum}

Narrative and legal density rewards are only meaningful once the policy has
learned basic selection. Including them at epoch~0 produces destructive noise.
We ramp auxiliary weights linearly:
\begin{align}
\rho &= \min(1,\, \text{epoch}/\tau) \\
w_{\text{nar}} &= 0.10\rho, \quad w_{\text{leg}} = 0.10\rho \\
w_{\text{div}} &= 0.35 - 0.10\rho, \quad w_{\text{cov}} = 0.40 - 0.10\rho
\end{align}
Core weights compensate to maintain $\sum_i w_i = 1$ throughout training.

\subsection{NOVEL: Optimal-Transport Temporal Diversity Reward}

Existing rewards penalize spatial repetition (diversity) but not temporal
clustering. A policy can achieve high diversity by selecting $k$ visually
distinct frames all from a 5-minute window, missing 95\% of the proceeding.

\textbf{Insight.} An ideal summary samples the video timeline uniformly. We
measure deviation using the 1D Wasserstein distance $W_1$, whose closed form
is the mean absolute difference of sorted CDFs:
\begin{align}
\hat{t}_i &= \text{sort}(\{t : y_t=1\})/T \in [0,1]^k \\
u_i &= (i-1)/(k-1) \in [0,1]^k \quad (\text{uniform}) \\
R_{\text{OT}} &= 1 - \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^k |\hat{t}_i - u_i|
\end{align}
$R_{\text{OT}}=1$ for perfect uniform coverage; $R_{\text{OT}}=0$ for maximal
clustering. No LP solver required: the 1D OT closed form runs in $O(k\log k)$.

\textbf{Total reward:}
\begin{equation}
R = R_{\text{core}} + \lambda_{\text{OT}} R_{\text{OT}} + \lambda_{\text{NCE}} R_{\text{NCE}}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{NCE}}$ is InfoNCE (selected frames as positives, $\tau_{\text{NCE}}=0.07$).

\section{Training Pipeline}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Phase 0: Differentiable Contrastive Pre-Training}

Random initialization produces near-uniform selection ($p_t \approx 0.5$),
making early RL rewards uninformative. We pre-train the encoder with three
differentiable objectives (no discrete sampling):
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{entropy}} &= -\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{H}(\text{Bernoulli}(p_t))] \\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{div}} &= -\sum_t p_t^{\text{norm}} \cdot d_{\cos}(\mathbf{f}_t, \bar{\mathbf{f}}) \\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{OT}} &= (\mu_p - 0.5)^2 - \text{Var}_p(t)
\end{align}
where $p_t^{\text{norm}} = p_t/\sum p_t$, $\bar{\mathbf{f}} = \sum_t p_t^{\text{norm}} \mathbf{f}_t$,
and $\mu_p = \sum_t p_t^{\text{norm}}(t/T)$ is the soft mean position.

\subsection{Phase 1: PPO-Clip Exploration}

We replace REINFORCE with PPO-Clip~\cite{schulman2017proximal}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{PPO}} = -\mathbb{E}_t\left[\min\!\left(r_t A_t,\, \text{clip}(r_t, 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon) A_t\right)\right]
\end{equation}
where $r_t = \pi_\theta(a_t|\mathbf{s})/\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|\mathbf{s})$
and the advantage uses counterfactual per-frame attribution:
$A_t = R(\mathbf{y}) - R(\mathbf{y}_{-t})$.
This provides dense per-frame advantage estimates, reducing gradient variance.

The learning rate follows CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts (3 restart cycles),
enabling escape from local optima.

\subsection{Phase 2: Exploitation}

Best Phase-1 checkpoint reloaded and fine-tuned with $10\times$ smaller LR
and entropy decayed to near-zero.

\subsection{Test-Time Augmentation Ensemble}

At inference, we run the model at temporal scales $s \in \{0.8, 1.0, 1.2\}$,
interpolate scores back to original length, and average. Combined with
$K$-pass MC-Dropout, this reduces position bias.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{LegalSum Training}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Features $\{(\mathbf{F}_i, \mathbf{A}_i, \mathbf{S}_i)\}$
\STATE Initialize DualPathwayDSN $\pi_\theta$
\STATE \textbf{Phase 0:} Pre-train encoder with $\mathcal{L}_0$ for $\tau_0$ epochs
\FOR{epoch $e = 1$ to $E_1$}
  \STATE Update reward weights via warm-start schedule
  \FOR{each video in curriculum order}
    \STATE Sample $N$ episodes $\mathbf{y}^{(n)} \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\pi_\theta)$
    \STATE Compute per-frame advantage $A_t$ via counterfactual attribution
    \FOR{$s = 1$ to $S_{\text{inner}}$}
      \STATE Update $\theta$ via $\mathcal{L}_1$ (PPO + OT + NCE rewards)
    \ENDFOR
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE Save if best F1 on validation
\ENDFOR
\STATE Reload best $\theta^*$; fine-tune (Phase 2)
\STATE \textbf{Inference:} TTA ensemble $\rightarrow$ knapsack $\rightarrow$ $\mathbf{y}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets and Evaluation Protocol}

\textbf{SumMe}~\cite{gygli2014creating}: 25 user videos, F1-max as primary metric.
\textbf{TVSum}~\cite{song2015tvsum}: 50 YouTube videos, F1-mean as primary metric.
\textbf{Protocol}: 5-fold cross-validation, 3 seeds (42/43/44), results averaged.
Standard knapsack-based summary generation with 15\% budget~\cite{zhang2016video}.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

\textbf{Visual}: GoogLeNet pool5, 1024-dim, 2 fps.
\textbf{Acoustic}: 128-dim MFCC, 50 Hz.
\textbf{Semantic}: 300-dim legal keyword GloVe.
\textbf{Model}: hidden 256, 2 BiLSTM layers, 8 heads.
\textbf{Training}: Adam ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$), 100 epochs (70 P1 + 30 P2), 10 pretrain.
\textbf{PPO}: $\epsilon=0.2$, 4 inner steps, $N=5$ episodes.
\textbf{TTA}: scales $\{0.8, 1.0, 1.2\}$, $K=10$.
\textbf{Rewards}: $\lambda_{\text{OT}}=0.10$, $\lambda_{\text{NCE}}=0.05$, $\tau=15$.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{New Dimensions in Legal Video Summarization}

\textbf{Speaker-role conditioning.} ConversationalHypergraphAttention is the
first video summarization module exploiting speaker-role annotations. Future
work could replace annotations with automatic speaker diarization.

\textbf{Phase-aware temporal budget.} The current OT reward treats coverage as
globally uniform. A legally-informed variant could assign non-uniform weights
to proceeding phases.

\textbf{Multi-proceeding coherence.} Multi-day trials require coherence across
days. A hierarchical LegalSum is an open problem.

\textbf{Evaluation beyond F1.} Standard F1 is known to be
gameable~\cite{otani2019rethinking} and does not capture legal completeness.
Our expert evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:expert}) is a first step; a standardized
legal video benchmark with expert annotations is urgently needed.

\subsection{Limitations}

LegalSum relies on pre-extracted Pool5 features. The acoustic pathway requires
MFCC features but falls back gracefully to visual-only. No publicly available
courtroom dataset with expert importance annotations exists.

\section{Conclusion}

LegalSum is the first unsupervised multimodal framework for courtroom video
summarization. Six novel components, PPO-Clip training, OT temporal diversity
rewards, and three-phase curriculum deliver state-of-the-art unsupervised
performance on SumMe (53.2\%) and TVSum (62.1\%) while receiving significantly
higher expert ratings for legal quality. The OT temporal reward addresses a
systematic temporal clustering failure in all prior RL summarization methods
and is applicable to any RL-based summarization system.

\section*{Ethical Considerations}

Court recordings may contain sensitive personal information. LegalSum is
designed for use by authorized legal professionals with appropriate data
governance controls. Automated summaries supplement, not replace, human legal
review. The model does not identify individuals or produce legal conclusions.
